# EK8818, Session 11 — Clinical Summaries

**Session**: 11 of 16 | **Patient**: young adult, MDD | **Working phase**: 3 segments

## Context before working phase

The patient is a young adult exploring a long-standing sense that she cannot feel emotions in ways she thinks are appropriate or expected, particularly that she does not cry in response to serious events (illness of friends' grandparents, Holocaust narratives, difficult experiences at her place of study). The session's working phase is centered on this theme: the absence of crying, and what that absence means to her.

---

## Segment 1

**Context.** The patient says that she is afraid that her good friend's grandmother will die and she did not know how to feel about it.

**Main topics discussed.** The patient shares her difficulty in feeling emotions, the emotional distance she experiences even when she is exposed to events that are difficult and sad, which should evoke corresponding emotions in her.

**Self-state(s).** The patient has an expectation that her competence needs will not be met, that she cannot feel the proper emotions, and identify with the pain of others. In response to this, she is critical of herself.

**Therapist interventions.** The therapist mainly asks exploratory questions to expand the patient's emotions and thoughts.

**Relationship.** The patient-therapist relationship appears to be positive and respectful. The therapist is actively attentive to the patient, and the patient responds to the expanding interventions that the therapist provides.

**Micro-level outcome.** The levels of insight in this segment are well, and the overall level of experiencing, while low, is relatively good.

## Segment 2

**Main topics discussed.** The patient's dominant self-state includes her wish to be able to express her emotions, while giving legitimacy to the expression of it. This is talked also in the context of the patient's trying to relate to others. The self-state that includes her expectations for her inability to express and connect emotionally, is also expressed in this segment but to a lesser extent.

**Self-state(s).** Same as above: two self-states are active, one leaning toward adaptive expression and legitimacy of feeling, the other toward the expectation that her emotional life cannot be shared.

## Segment 3

**Main topics discussed.** The patient presents situations in which she feels the need to lighten the atmosphere or find a compromise. When other people are very sad or angry she feels very uncomfortable and wants to change the atmosphere. The patient states that this desire probably comes from her father.

**Self-state(s).** The patient desires the ability to assist people, resolve conflicts, and offer support during challenging times. It appears that sometimes the other accepts her help, while at other times, they block it. Situations where people are sad or angry make her discomfort and a tendency in her to immediately lighten the atmosphere through a fight reaction.

**Therapist interventions.** The therapist asks exploratory questions to dig deeper into the patient's emotions and thoughts, helping to give meaning to her feelings and reactions, reflecting her desires and connecting current experiences with past ones.

**Relationship.** The patient-therapist relationship appears to be quite positive and open; the therapist is actively attentive to the patient, supports her, and the patient responds to the expanding interventions that the therapist provides.

**Micro-level outcome.** The levels of regulation and insight of the patient in this segment are well, and the overall level of well-being is relatively good.
